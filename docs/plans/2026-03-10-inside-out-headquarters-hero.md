# Inside Out: Headquarters Hero — Full Game Design Document

---

## 1. ONE-PARAGRAPH PITCH

Inside Out: Headquarters Hero is a fast, funny, and emotionally rich mobile game where Arthur becomes the trusted hero inside Riley's mind. When a mysterious chain reaction sends Headquarters into chaos — control panels overloading, memory pipes cracking, alarm systems spiraling — the emotions call on Arthur to rush room to room, solving playful emergencies with quick touch gameplay. Each room is a distinct emotional zone with its own visual style, characters, and mini-games: cool down Anger's overheating reactor, sort through Anxiety's spiraling alarms, rescue lost memories from crumbling vaults, and fix Dream Studio's runaway productions. The game is built around stacking crises that chain together — fixing one problem reveals another, creating urgency, surprise, and a powerful hero fantasy. Arthur restores Headquarters piece by piece, unlocking new rooms, earning collectibles, and watching the world visibly transform from broken to beautiful. It is a game that feels like a rescue adventure inside a living world, not a lesson — fast enough to play in two minutes, deep enough to come back every day.

---

## 2. CORE FANTASY

### Why a child wants to play
The emotions from Inside Out are panicking. Headquarters is falling apart. Sirens are blaring, pipes are leaking glowing memories, Anger is literally on fire, and Anxiety's screens are flashing warnings everywhere. Only Arthur can fix it. This is the ultimate helper fantasy: every character needs you, every room is broken, and only your hands on the screen can make things right. Children love being needed, and this game makes them feel indispensable.

### Why Arthur matters
Arthur is not an emotion — he is the special helper the emotions trust because he can do what they cannot. Joy gets too excited and makes things worse. Anger overreacts. Fear hides. Anxiety overthinks. Arthur is the calm, capable hero who walks into every disaster and actually fixes it. The player IS Arthur — and every successful repair earns gratitude, celebration, and visible world improvement. Arthur grows from a new helper to the legendary Headquarters Hero.

### What makes the world exciting
Headquarters is a living place. Rooms hum, glow, spark, and breathe. When things break, they break spectacularly — memory orbs roll across floors, dream projectors play backwards, warning lights flash in cascading patterns, steam vents pop open. When Arthur fixes things, the world responds just as dramatically — systems power up with satisfying whooshes, colors brighten, characters cheer, and broken architecture rebuilds itself with cinematic flair. The world is always reacting to what the player does.

### What makes this feel bigger than a normal mini-game app
Three things: the chain reaction system (one problem causes another, creating an unfolding story), the persistent world restoration (every repair changes Headquarters permanently), and the character relationships (each emotion remembers what Arthur did and reacts to his growing reputation). This is not a menu of disconnected games — it is a world that evolves, breaks, heals, and surprises.

## 3. CORE GAMEPLAY LOOP

### The Repeatable Loop

**Step 1 — Alert.**
The Headquarters map shows a flashing emergency icon on one of the rooms. A quick animated popup shows what is going wrong — Anger's reactor is overheating, or memory orbs are leaking, or Anxiety's alarms are cascading. An emotion character appears in a speech bubble with a panicked one-liner (voiced, minimal text). The emergency feels urgent but playful, never scary.

**Step 2 — Enter.**
Arthur taps the flashing room. A fast zoom-in transition takes Arthur into the room. The room is visually damaged — cracked panels, floating debris, flickering lights, steam, scattered objects. The emotion who lives there is reacting to the chaos. The mini-game objective appears as a simple visual icon (no text required).

**Step 3 — Play.**
Arthur plays a 30-to-90-second mini-game using touch controls — tapping, dragging, swiping, holding, sorting, dodging, or tracing. The game starts easy and escalates. Visual and audio feedback is constant and satisfying — sparkle trails, pop sounds, whoosh effects, character reactions. The emotion comments and reacts throughout (voiced, short, funny).

**Step 4 — Succeed or Retry.**
On success: a big celebration — the room visually repairs, the emotion cheers, confetti or energy bursts fill the screen, and a reward pops up. On failure: the emotion reacts with humor (Anger fumes, Anxiety panics more, Fear faints), the room stays damaged, and a friendly retry button appears. Failure is funny, not punishing.

**Step 5 — Reward.**
Arthur earns a reward: a memory orb, a room repair piece, a sticker, a character reaction, or a rare collectible. The reward is shown with a satisfying presentation — it flies into Arthur's collection with a trail of particles.

**Step 6 — World Update.**
Back on the Headquarters map, the repaired room looks slightly better. If this was the last repair needed, the room transforms with a cinematic upgrade animation. New emergency alerts may have appeared on other rooms — especially if the chain reaction system has triggered. The child sees new things to do immediately.

**Step 7 — Loop.**
The player taps the next emergency and the loop restarts. If no emergencies are active, the player can replay rooms for better scores or explore for hidden collectibles. Daily events and boss events provide fresh content.

### What keeps the loop satisfying
- Every session changes the world visibly
- Rewards feel tangible and varied
- Chain reactions create surprise (fixing one room may break another)
- Character reactions are funny and personal
- The loop is fast — a full cycle takes 60-120 seconds
- There is always something new to fix

## 4. MAIN SYSTEMS

### 4A. Progression System
**What it is:** A linear unlock path through Headquarters. Arthur starts with access to the Control Room only. Each room repair milestone unlocks the next room.
**How it works:** Completing emergencies earns Restoration Stars. Accumulate enough stars and the next room opens with a cinematic unlock sequence where the emotion who lives there greets Arthur.
**Why it is fun:** Every unlock feels like discovering a new area of a world. The unlock animations are big, exciting moments.
**Why kids come back:** They want to see what the next room looks like and who lives there.

### 4B. Room Restoration
**What it is:** Each room has 5 visual upgrade stages: Destroyed → Damaged → Functional → Polished → Spectacular. Each stage changes the room's appearance, adds decorative details, and unlocks new interactions.
**How it works:** Completing mini-games in a room earns Repair Parts. Enough parts trigger the next restoration stage with a cinematic transformation.
**Why it is fun:** Watching a broken, flickering room transform into a gleaming, animated masterpiece is deeply satisfying. Each stage adds visible details — working screens, glowing pathways, decorative elements, character furniture.
**Why kids come back:** They want to see their rooms fully restored and compare before/after states.

### 4C. Collectibles — Memory Orb Collection
**What it is:** Glowing colored orbs that represent emotional memories. Each orb has a color (matching its emotion) and a tiny animated scene inside it.
**How it works:** Earned from mini-games, daily events, and hidden in rooms. Collected orbs fill a Memory Shelf in a dedicated collection screen. Complete sets unlock bonus content.
**Why it is fun:** The orbs are beautiful, and each one contains a unique micro-animation. Collecting feels like building a treasure museum.
**Why kids come back:** Incomplete collections create gentle desire to fill gaps. Rare orbs (gold, rainbow) are especially desirable.

### 4D. Daily Events — Emergency of the Day
**What it is:** Every day, a unique emergency appears that was not available yesterday. It might affect a specific room in a new way, introduce a temporary mini-game variant, or feature an emotion doing something unusual.
**How it works:** On app open, the daily event appears as a special flashing alert with a unique border color. Completing it gives a daily-exclusive reward.
**Why it is fun:** It creates a "what's new today?" feeling every time the child opens the game.
**Why kids come back:** Fear of missing the daily reward plus curiosity about what today's emergency will be.

### 4E. Chain Reaction System
**What it is:** The central hook. Emergencies in one room can trigger problems in connected rooms. Fixing Anger's overheating reactor might cause a steam pipe to burst in the Memory Vault. Calming Anxiety's alarms might accidentally wake up something in the Dream Studio.
**How it works:** After completing certain mini-games, a Chain Reaction Alert plays: an animated sequence shows the consequence spreading to another room. That room now has a new emergency. Some chain reactions are scripted (story-driven), others are semi-random (for replayability).
**Why it is fun:** It creates an unfolding story. The child feels like they are living through an adventure, not just playing isolated games. The urgency of "oh no, it spread!" is thrilling.
**Why kids come back:** Chain reactions create unpredictable sequences. The same starting emergency can lead to different chains on different days.

### 4F. Emergency Stacking
**What it is:** Multiple rooms can be in crisis simultaneously. When 3+ rooms are flashing, Headquarters enters Yellow Alert. When 5+ rooms are in crisis, it enters Red Alert. The map becomes more dramatic — warning lights, klaxon sounds, emotions running across the map in panic.
**How it works:** Emergencies accumulate if the player does not address them. Stacked emergencies increase the reward multiplier — fixing a room during Red Alert gives bonus rewards.
**Why it is fun:** It creates real tension and makes the player feel heroic for tackling multiple crises. The escalating visual drama on the map is exciting.
**Why kids come back:** Red Alert mode feels epic and special. The bonus rewards incentivize letting things stack (a deliberate strategic choice).

### 4G. Hero Tools
**What it is:** Arthur unlocks special abilities as he progresses.
**Tools include:**
- **Calm Pulse** — A radial wave that temporarily slows chaos in a mini-game. Earned after restoring 2 rooms.
- **Memory Magnet** — Attracts loose memory orbs toward Arthur. Earned after restoring the Memory Vault.
- **Freeze Burst** — Temporarily freezes overheating or moving objects. Earned after the first boss event.
- **Joy Boost** — Supercharges Arthur's actions for 5 seconds, making taps/swipes more powerful. Earned from Joy after a major milestone.
**How it works:** Tools recharge between mini-games. The child taps a tool icon during gameplay to activate it. Each tool has a flashy activation animation.
**Why it is fun:** Tools feel like superpowers. Using them at the right moment is satisfying.
**Why kids come back:** New tools change how old mini-games feel, creating replay value.

### 4H. Boss Events
**What it is:** Major multi-room disasters that occur at progression milestones. A boss event is not a single villain but a cascading emotional mega-crisis that affects multiple rooms simultaneously and requires the player to fix several mini-games in sequence.
**How it works:** Boss events are announced with a cinematic intro. The player must complete 3-4 mini-games across different rooms in a specific order to resolve the crisis. Between each mini-game, a short animated sequence shows the chain reaction progressing.
**Why it is fun:** Boss events feel like movie climaxes. The stakes are higher, the visuals are more dramatic, and the final resolution is the biggest celebration in the game.
**Why kids come back:** Boss events unlock the most valuable rewards and the most spectacular room transformations.

### 4I. Character Unlock Moments
**What it is:** Emotions start locked behind their room doors. Unlocking a new room means meeting a new character for the first time.
**How it works:** When the player accumulates enough Restoration Stars, a new room unlocks. A short animated cutscene plays: the door opens, the emotion stumbles out in distress, sees Arthur, and reacts with relief. From that point, the emotion appears throughout the game — on the map, in other rooms during chain reactions, and in the collection screen.
**Why it is fun:** Meeting a new character is a major event. Each emotion has a unique, memorable introduction.
**Why kids come back:** They want to unlock all the emotions and see all the introductions.

### 4J. Cosmetic Personalization
**What it is:** Arthur can earn decorative items for Headquarters and for himself.
**Items include:** Room wallpapers, floor patterns, light colors, emotion furniture, Arthur's helper badge designs, map decorations.
**How it works:** Earned from achievements, daily events, and rare drops. Applied from a simple customization screen.
**Why it is fun:** Children love making things "theirs." Seeing their personalized version of Headquarters feels special.
**Why kids come back:** New cosmetics rotate in daily events, creating ongoing desire.

### 4K. Streaks and Return Incentives
**What it is:** Playing on consecutive days builds a streak. Each day of the streak increases the daily reward and adds a visual streak counter on the map (a growing row of glowing stars).
**How it works:** Day 1 = 1 memory orb. Day 2 = 2 orbs. Day 3 = 3 orbs + a rare sticker. Day 5 = bonus hero tool charge. Day 7 = rare golden memory orb.
**Why it is fun:** The escalating rewards feel earned. The visual streak counter is satisfying to grow.
**Why kids come back:** Breaking a streak feels wasteful. The day 7 reward is highly desirable.

### 4L. Surprise Disruptions
**What it is:** Occasionally, without warning, something unexpected happens. A memory orb rolls across the Headquarters map and the player must tap it before it disappears. An emotion runs through screaming and drops a sticker. A pipe bursts on the map screen and the player must plug it.
**How it works:** Random micro-events on the map screen between mini-games. They last 3-5 seconds and reward quick reaction.
**Why it is fun:** They break the pattern and create delight. The unpredictability keeps the map screen interesting.
**Why kids come back:** They want to catch rare surprise events.

## 5. ROOM-BY-ROOM DESIGN

### Room 1: Control Room

**Emotional Purpose:** The emotional command center — where all feelings are managed and balanced.
**Fantasy:** The main control console has gone haywire. Buttons are flashing wrong colors, levers are stuck, screens are showing static, and the emotion controls are scrambled.
**Visual Identity:** A large semicircular console desk facing a massive window that looks out into Riley's mind. Banks of colorful buttons, levers, sliders, and screens. Think NASA mission control meets a candy-colored synthesizer.
**Color and Mood:** Warm yellows, soft purples, and cool blues when functional. Flashing red, orange, and white when in crisis. The room should feel like the heart of Headquarters.
**Kind of Chaos:** System overloads. Buttons firing randomly. Screens displaying wrong emotions. Levers snapping to wrong positions. The console sparking and beeping.
**Main Mechanic:** Tap-and-drag repair. The player must tap flashing buttons to reset them, drag levers to correct positions, and swipe screens to clear static.
**What Arthur Does:** Stands at the console and physically interacts with each control. His hands appear on screen for each action.
**Escalation:** Starts with one or two malfunctioning controls. Progresses to entire banks of controls going haywire simultaneously. At maximum difficulty, controls reset themselves if not fixed fast enough.
**Secret Emotional Teaching:** Emotional regulation — the idea that feelings need to be managed, not suppressed. Balancing the controls mirrors balancing emotions.
**Sample Challenge:** Five emotion buttons are stuck on the wrong color. Drag each button's color slider to match the correct emotion color before the timer bar empties. Each correct match triggers a satisfying click and glow.
**Sample Fail State:** The console overloads completely — all screens go to static, a comedic alarm sounds, and Joy facepalms while the console powers down with a sad descending tone. A friendly "Try Again!" button pulses.
**Sample Win Moment:** All controls snap into place with a cascading click-click-click. The massive window lights up with a beautiful view of Riley's mind. Joy cheers and the console hums with warm, stable light.
**Sample Reward:** A Control Room memory orb (yellow with a tiny working console inside it).
**Why This Room is Memorable:** It is the command center — fixing it feels like saving the whole operation. The console is tactile and satisfying to interact with.

### Room 2: Anger Reactor

**Emotional Purpose:** The source of Anger's energy — a volcanic power core that generates emotional intensity.
**Fantasy:** The reactor is overheating. Lava is bubbling up, pipes are glowing red-hot, pressure gauges are in the danger zone, and steam vents are popping open everywhere.
**Visual Identity:** An industrial boiler room meets a cartoon volcano. Massive pipes, pressure dials, glowing red-orange energy conduits, a central reactor core that pulses like a heartbeat. Heavy riveted metal, steam clouds, and heat shimmer effects.
**Color and Mood:** Deep reds, hot oranges, molten yellows when overheating. Cool steel blues, warm ambers, and steady greens when stable. The room should feel powerful and intense but never threatening.
**Kind of Chaos:** Overheating. Pressure building. Pipes bursting. Lava bubbling over. Temperature gauges maxing out. Steam clouds obscuring the view.
**Main Mechanic:** Cool-down actions. Swipe ice patches onto hot surfaces. Tap pressure valves to release steam. Drag cooling rods into the reactor core. Hold fire extinguishers on flame spots.
**What Arthur Does:** Runs around the reactor with cooling tools, physically interacting with overheating equipment.
**Escalation:** Starts with one hot pipe. Progresses to multiple simultaneous overheating systems. At maximum difficulty, cooled pipes reheat if neglected, and new hot spots appear faster than they can be cooled.
**Secret Emotional Teaching:** Anger management — the idea that anger builds up and needs healthy release. Cooling the reactor mirrors calming down. The pressure valves teach that releasing anger in small, controlled bursts prevents explosions.
**Sample Challenge:** Three pipes are glowing red. Swipe ice patches onto each pipe before the temperature gauge hits the top. New pipes heat up as old ones cool. Cool all pipes within 45 seconds.
**Sample Fail State:** The reactor erupts with a cartoonish volcanic belch. Anger emerges covered in soot, steam coming from his head, grumbling "I TOLD you it was getting hot in here!" The room fills with harmless orange foam.
**Sample Win Moment:** The reactor settles to a gentle blue glow. Pressure gauges all read green. Anger sighs with relief, his flame shrinks to a tiny flicker, and he gives Arthur a gruff thumbs-up.
**Sample Reward:** A Reactor Core fragment (glowing red-orange crystal that pulses).
**Why This Room is Memorable:** The heat, the urgency, the satisfying cool-down. It is visceral and physical. Anger's reactions are always funny.

---

### Room 3: Anxiety Forecast Dome

**Emotional Purpose:** Anxiety's prediction and warning center — a room filled with screens, projections, and probability calculations that monitor everything that could go wrong.
**Fantasy:** The forecasting systems are spiraling. Every screen is showing worst-case scenarios. Alarm bells are ringing in cascading waves. Warning notifications are multiplying faster than they can be dismissed. The whole dome is in a state of information overload.
**Visual Identity:** A planetarium-like dome ceiling covered in holographic screens, data streams, and floating notification bubbles. Think weather center meets social media notification overload. Swirling teal and orange holograms. Scrolling text tickers. Flashing warning icons everywhere.
**Color and Mood:** Teal, orange, and white when active. Deep teal, soft lavender, and gentle amber when calm. In crisis: harsh flashing teal, overwhelming orange, screens multiplying.
**Kind of Chaos:** Information overload. Too many alarms. Screens multiplying. Forecasts getting more catastrophic. Notification bubbles spawning endlessly.
**Main Mechanic:** Sort-and-dismiss. The player must identify real problems vs. false alarms, sort notifications into correct categories, and dismiss unnecessary warnings by swiping them away. Tap real issues to address them, swipe false alarms offscreen.
**What Arthur Does:** Stands in the center of the dome, reaching up to interact with floating holographic screens and notification bubbles.
**Escalation:** Starts with a few floating notifications, clearly marked real vs. false. Progresses to many notifications at once, some ambiguous. At maximum difficulty, dismissed false alarms sometimes return, and real issues flash urgently.
**Secret Emotional Teaching:** Anxiety management — distinguishing real concerns from worry spirals. Sorting real vs. false alarms mirrors identifying which anxious thoughts deserve attention and which can be let go.
**Sample Challenge:** Twelve notification bubbles float around the dome. Eight are false alarms (orange border, silly messages like "What if clouds are angry?"), four are real issues (teal border, like "Memory pipe leak detected"). Swipe away the false alarms and tap the real issues within 40 seconds.
**Sample Fail State:** The dome fills completely with notification bubbles until the screen is covered. Anxiety appears buried under a pile of sticky notes, eyes wide, saying "See?! I TOLD you everything was going wrong!" All bubbles pop harmlessly and float away like confetti.
**Sample Win Moment:** All false alarms dissolve with satisfying pops. The real issues flash green as "resolved." The dome clears to show a beautiful, calm starfield projection. Anxiety takes a deep breath and smiles nervously.
**Sample Reward:** A Forecast Crystal (teal orb with tiny swirling data streams inside).
**Why This Room is Memorable:** The visual spectacle of a dome filled with floating holograms. The satisfying rhythm of sorting and dismissing. Anxiety's relatable overreaction.

---

### Room 4: Memory Vault

**Emotional Purpose:** The archive of all memories — a vast, beautiful library-warehouse of glowing memory orbs stored on towering shelves.
**Fantasy:** The vault's shelving system has collapsed. Memory orbs are rolling everywhere, falling off shelves, mixing up, and some are cracking and leaking emotional color. The organizational system is destroyed.
**Visual Identity:** A massive cathedral-like space with towering shelves reaching into darkness above. Thousands of glowing orbs in every color line the shelves. Conveyor belts, pneumatic tube systems, and robotic sorting arms. Think the Hogwarts library crossed with a magical Amazon warehouse.
**Color and Mood:** Warm golden light from the orbs, deep purple shadows, soft glowing colors everywhere. In crisis: orbs scattered on the ground like marbles, shelves tilted, conveyor belts jammed, flickering overhead lights.
**Kind of Chaos:** Structural collapse. Orbs rolling free. Mixed-up memories. Broken sorting systems. Lost memories fading.
**Main Mechanic:** Sort-and-shelve. Drag memory orbs to matching color-coded shelf sections. Rebuild collapsed shelves by tapping scaffold points. Rescue fading memories by tracing their outlines before they disappear.
**What Arthur Does:** Catches rolling orbs, lifts them onto shelves, repairs broken conveyors, and traces fading memories to save them.
**Escalation:** Starts with a few orbs to sort into obvious color groups. Progresses to many orbs at once with more color categories. At maximum difficulty, orbs roll toward exits and must be caught before they escape, while new orbs keep falling.
**Secret Emotional Teaching:** Memory and emotional categorization — understanding that memories carry feelings, and that organizing our experiences helps us understand ourselves. Sorting by color mirrors identifying the emotion in a memory.
**Sample Challenge:** Fifteen memory orbs are rolling across the floor. Sort them into five color-coded bins (yellow/joy, blue/sadness, red/anger, purple/fear, green/disgust) by dragging each orb to the correct bin. Orbs roll faster over time.
**Sample Fail State:** Too many orbs escape through the vault door. Sadness appears and cradles one remaining orb, looking up at Arthur with huge eyes: "Do you think... they'll come back?" A gentle chime plays and a "Try Again?" prompt appears.
**Sample Win Moment:** All orbs snap onto shelves with a cascading musical tone (each color plays a different note, creating a chord). The vault lights up in a rainbow sweep. The shelves straighten themselves. The sorting system whirs back to life.
**Sample Reward:** A Rare Memory Orb (multicolored, contains a tiny animated scene).
**Why This Room is Memorable:** The sheer beauty of thousands of glowing orbs. The tactile satisfaction of sorting. The musical feedback when orbs are placed correctly.

---

### Room 5: Dream Studio

**Emotional Purpose:** Where dreams are produced — a movie studio that creates Riley's nightly dream experiences.
**Fantasy:** The dream projectors are malfunctioning. Dreams are playing backwards, mixing together, or creating absurd combinations. Film reels are tangled. Sets are collapsing. Props are in the wrong scenes.
**Visual Identity:** A classic Hollywood sound stage merged with a surrealist art studio. Spotlights, film reels, director's chairs, green screens showing impossible landscapes, prop closets overflowing with bizarre items. Painted backdrops lean against walls. Small dream characters mill about in confusion.
**Color and Mood:** Warm spotlights, purple and magenta dream haze, gold and silver metallic studio equipment. In crisis: flickering projectors casting wild shadows, dreams overlapping and phasing in and out, colors bleeding between scenes.
**Kind of Chaos:** Production chaos. Wrong props in wrong scenes. Tangled film reels. Projectors showing mixed-up dreams. Sets collapsing. Dream characters wandering into wrong scenes.
**Main Mechanic:** Rebuild and match. Drag props to the correct dream scene. Untangle film reels by tracing paths. Match dream characters to their correct stages.
**What Arthur Does:** Works as a dream studio assistant — organizing props, fixing projectors, directing dream characters to the right places.
**Escalation:** Starts with one malfunctioning dream scene to fix. Progresses to multiple scenes running simultaneously. At maximum difficulty, fixing one scene causes another to malfunction, and dream characters actively wander away from correct positions.
**Secret Emotional Teaching:** Creative expression and processing — understanding that dreams help us process experiences. Organizing dream content mirrors making sense of complex feelings.
**Sample Challenge:** Three dream stages are running the wrong scenes. Stage A needs the cloud props (currently on Stage C). Stage B needs the rainbow characters (currently on Stage A). Stage C needs the ocean backdrop (currently on Stage B). Drag all props and characters to correct stages before the "Director Cuts!" timer expires.
**Sample Fail State:** All dreams merge into one absurd super-dream: a fish riding a bicycle through a cloud made of pizza while a dinosaur plays violin. The dream audience (tiny cloud people) look confused. A clapperboard snaps shut with "TAKE 2?"
**Sample Win Moment:** Each dream stage lights up with a perfectly running dream sequence. The projectors beam beautiful coordinated imagery. A tiny audience of dream viewers cheers. The director's chair spins with a golden star.
**Sample Reward:** A Dream Reel (a tiny golden film reel that plays a micro-dream animation).
**Why This Room is Memorable:** The absurdity of malfunctioning dreams is inherently funny. The surrealist visuals are the most creative in the game. Props are silly and surprising.

### Room 6: Fear Maze

**Emotional Purpose:** Fear's defense system — a maze of corridors, locked doors, and safety barriers designed to protect Riley from perceived threats.
**Fantasy:** Fear has locked down too many passages. Emergency barriers have slammed shut everywhere. The safety systems have gone into overdrive and now nothing can get through — including the things that need to.
**Visual Identity:** A labyrinth of corridors with heavy blast doors, yellow-and-black hazard stripes, flashing warning lights, and security cameras. Think submarine interior meets theme park haunted house (but funny, not scary). Purple emergency lighting. Thick metal doors with oversized locks.
**Color and Mood:** Deep purple, yellow hazard stripes, flashing amber lights. Security green for unlocked areas. In crisis: everything locked, purple emergency lighting, barriers slamming shut.
**Kind of Chaos:** Over-lockdown. Too many barriers. Passages blocked. Items trapped behind doors. Fear hiding behind increasingly ridiculous barricades.
**Main Mechanic:** Unlock-and-navigate. Solve simple lock puzzles (match shapes, turn keys, enter color codes) to open doors. Trace paths through the maze to find the correct route. Tap barriers to lower them.
**What Arthur Does:** Walks through corridors, solving lock puzzles at each door, reassuring Fear that it is safe to open up.
**Escalation:** Starts with one locked door with a simple shape-match lock. Progresses to multiple doors with different lock types in sequence. At maximum difficulty, opened doors start relocking behind Arthur, and Fear adds extra barriers mid-puzzle.
**Secret Emotional Teaching:** Courage and safety assessment — understanding that fear tries to protect us but can overdo it. Opening locked doors mirrors facing fears with courage, proving that the imagined threat is manageable.
**Sample Challenge:** A corridor has four locked doors. Door 1: match three shapes. Door 2: turn three keys in the right order (shown briefly). Door 3: drag a color to complete a pattern. Door 4: hold a button for 3 seconds while Fear protests (funny). Complete all four within 50 seconds.
**Sample Fail State:** Fear slams one final enormous door shut and peers through a tiny window at Arthur: "Sorry! Too dangerous! Maybe tomorrow? Or never? Never's good too!" The door has seventeen locks on it.
**Sample Win Moment:** The final door opens to reveal a beautiful, safe room with warm golden light. Fear cautiously steps out and looks around in wonder: "Oh... it's actually nice in here." All barriers retract with satisfying mechanical sounds.
**Sample Reward:** A Courage Key (a glowing golden key collectible).
**Why This Room is Memorable:** The maze structure creates a sense of adventure and exploration unlike any other room. Fear's exaggerated reactions are comedy gold. The locked-door mechanic is inherently satisfying.

### Room 7: Embarrassment Hideout

**Emotional Purpose:** Embarrassment's personal retreat — a cozy, hidden room where embarrassing moments are managed (and avoided).
**Fantasy:** Embarrassment has hidden everything. Objects are buried under blankets, shoved behind furniture, and stuffed in closets. The room itself is trying to make itself invisible — lights dimming, walls closing in, everything shrinking.
**Visual Identity:** A cozy bedroom-like space with oversized furniture, soft pink lighting, thick curtains drawn over everything, piles of blankets and cushions used to hide things. Think teenager's bedroom meets pillow fort. Warm, soft, and cluttered.
**Color and Mood:** Soft pink, warm mauve, cozy amber lighting. Lots of fabric textures — velvet, fleece, knit. In crisis: everything hidden, lights too dim, the room feels cramped because so much is stuffed under covers.
**Kind of Chaos:** Hiding and avoidance. Everything important is buried or concealed. The room is shrinking because so much is being hidden. Systems cannot function because components are missing (hidden under blankets).
**Main Mechanic:** Uncover-and-reveal. Drag blankets off objects. Open closet doors. Pull curtains back. Tap hidden objects to coax them out. Each revealed item brightens the room slightly.
**What Arthur Does:** Gently uncovers hidden items, reassuring each one that it is okay to be seen. Opens curtains and doors to let light in.
**Escalation:** Starts with a few obviously hidden items (shapes visible under blankets). Progresses to items hidden in less obvious places. At maximum difficulty, Embarrassment actively re-hides items that Arthur has uncovered, requiring faster action.
**Secret Emotional Teaching:** Vulnerability and self-acceptance — understanding that hiding from embarrassment makes things worse, and that bringing things into the open is freeing. Each uncovered item mirrors accepting an awkward moment.
**Sample Challenge:** Eight important items are hidden around the room — under blankets, behind curtains, inside drawers, under pillows. Find and uncover all eight. Each uncovered item makes the room brighter and bigger. Embarrassment hides behind a large pillow and peeks out after each reveal.
**Sample Fail State:** The room dims completely. Embarrassment has built a fort of blankets so tall it fills the screen. A tiny voice from inside says "I'll just... stay in here forever." A glowing "Try Again?" button appears outside the fort.
**Sample Win Moment:** The room fills with warm light. All items are visible and arranged nicely. The room expands to its full size. Embarrassment emerges, blushing but smiling, and waves shyly at Arthur.
**Sample Reward:** A Hidden Gem (a pink crystal that was under a blanket — it sparkles when uncovered).
**Why This Room is Memorable:** The warmth and coziness. The gentle humor of Embarrassment's hiding behavior. The satisfying visual progression from dark and cramped to bright and spacious.

### Room 8: Mixed Feelings Lab

**Emotional Purpose:** Where complex emotions are created by combining basic ones — the experimental research wing of Headquarters.
**Fantasy:** The mixing equipment has gone haywire. Emotions are combining in wrong proportions, creating unstable emotional compounds. Beakers are bubbling over, conveyor belts are crossing, and the resulting mixed emotions are volatile and unpredictable.
**Visual Identity:** A mad scientist's laboratory meets a paint mixing studio. Huge beakers filled with colored liquids (each color = an emotion), conveyor belts carrying emotion ingredients, mixing chambers with spinning centrifuges, and test tube racks. Bubbling sounds, steam, and swirling color combinations everywhere.
**Color and Mood:** All colors of the emotional spectrum, constantly mixing and shifting. Chrome and glass laboratory equipment. In crisis: colors clashing violently, beakers overflowing, sparks flying from bad combinations.
**Kind of Chaos:** Bad mixes. Wrong proportions. Volatile combinations. Overflowing beakers. Conveyor belts sending wrong ingredients to wrong mixers.
**Main Mechanic:** Combine-and-balance. Drag emotion colors into mixing beakers at the right proportions. Follow simple recipes (shown as color icons). Stop beakers from overflowing by tapping the release valve at the right moment.
**What Arthur Does:** Works as a lab assistant, following emotion recipes, operating mixing equipment, and stabilizing bad combinations.
**Escalation:** Starts with simple two-color mixes. Progresses to three-color mixes with timing requirements. At maximum difficulty, multiple beakers need attention simultaneously, and some mixes are volatile (the beaker shakes and must be tapped to stabilize).
**Secret Emotional Teaching:** Emotional complexity — understanding that feelings are rarely simple. We can feel happy and sad at the same time. Mixing colors mirrors the concept of mixed emotions, and getting the right balance mirrors emotional intelligence.
**Sample Challenge:** Three beakers need filling. Beaker A needs mostly yellow (joy) with a dash of blue (sadness) — this is "Nostalgia."
